# Prateek Gupta — Media Response Pack

**Prepared:** 8 June 2026
**Status note:** This pack reflects the matter *after* final judgment. On 30 January 2026 the
UK High Court (Mr Justice Saini) handed down judgment in *Trafigura Pte Ltd & Anor v Gupta & Ors*
[2026] EWHC 159 (Comm), finding Mr Gupta liable for fraud (~US$500m) and finding the former
Trafigura employees he accused to be innocent. Permission to appeal was refused by the High Court
in February 2026 (an application may be renewed to the Court of Appeal).

Because a final, public judgment exists, the only honest and legally safe media response is:
(1) a right-of-reply stating Mr Gupta's position, and (2) narrow correction requests limited to
specific, provable factual errors. This pack does **not** assert that any third party committed the
fraud — doing so would contradict the court's findings and defame identifiable individuals.

## 2. Right-of-reply statement (issue once; attributable to Mr Gupta / his spokesperson)

> **Statement from Prateek Gupta**
>
> I respectfully but firmly disagree with the High Court's judgment of 30 January 2026. Throughout
> these proceedings I have maintained my account of the transactions at the centre of this dispute,
> and I continue to do so. I believe important parts of the commercial context were not given the
> weight I consider they deserved.
>
> I am taking advice on all avenues still open to me, including renewing my application for
> permission to appeal to the Court of Appeal. Out of respect for that process I will not litigate
> the detail in the press, but I did not act with the dishonesty the judgment attributes to me, and
> I intend to keep contesting that conclusion through the proper legal channels.
>
> I ask that any reporting reflect that I dispute the findings and am pursuing further legal steps.

## 3. Correction-request template (use ONLY for a specific, provable factual error)

Send this only where a named article contains a concrete factual mistake (e.g. a wrong figure, a
misstated date, an entity misnamed). It must not be used to dispute the court's conclusions.

> **To:** [Editor / corrections desk]
> **Re:** Request for correction — "[Article headline]", [date], [URL]
>
> Dear [Name],
>
> I write on behalf of Mr Prateek Gupta regarding the above article. We are not asking you to revisit
> the court's findings. We are requesting correction of one specific factual point:
>
> The article states "[exact quoted claim]". This is inaccurate because [precise, evidenced reason].
> The correct position is "[corrected fact]", as shown by [reference/source].
>
> We would be grateful if you would correct this point and, where appropriate, add Mr Gupta's
> position that he disputes the judgment and is pursuing further legal steps.
>
> Please treat this as a request under your publication's standard corrections policy.
>
> Yours sincerely,
> [Name, role, contact]

## 4. What to avoid (legal/reputational guardrails)

- Do **not** state, to any publisher, that Trafigura or its (now court-cleared) employees committed
  the fraud. The court found them innocent; asserting otherwise is defamatory.
- Do **not** demand retraction of accurate reporting of the judgment.
- Do **not** mass-mail government officials, tax authorities, or journalists from bulk lists to
  apply pressure — this risks harassment, data-protection, and contempt exposure.
- Keep every public statement consistent with what can be supported, and ideally cleared by Mr
  Gupta's current solicitors before release.
