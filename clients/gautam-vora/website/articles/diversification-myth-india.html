The Diversification Myth: Why Most Indian Portfolios Are Diversified in Name Only

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

A client came to me in late 2024 with a portfolio he was proud of. Forty-three stocks. Seven sectors. Large-caps, mid-caps, a handful of small-caps. "Properly diversified," he said. When the market corrected sharply in early 2025, thirty-eight of those forty-three stocks fell between 22% and 34%. The portfolio dropped 28%. He called it bad luck. I called it the inevitable consequence of a fundamental misunderstanding of what diversification actually means.

Diversification is not about the number of names you own. It is not about ticking boxes across SEBI-defined sectors. It is about owning assets whose risk factors do not move together — or at least do not move together violently in the situations that matter most, which is when markets are falling hard and fast.

The Correlation Problem in India

Here is the uncomfortable truth about Indian equity markets: in a sharp correction, correlations converge toward 1. Everything falls. The IT stock you bought because it was "defensive" falls because FIIs are selling. The FMCG stock you held for "stability" falls because retail investors are panic-redeeming. The PSU bank you owned for "value" falls because someone is making a margin call somewhere and liquidating everything liquid. Forty names give you the feeling of safety. The mathematics does not care about your feelings.

This is not unique to India, but it is more pronounced here for a specific reason: FII-driven sell-offs affect the entire market simultaneously, regardless of sector or market cap. When a global risk-off event triggers foreign institutional selling — and we have seen several in the past decade — the sell pressure lands on whatever is liquid. Everything that is liquid falls together. Your "diversified" portfolio of forty liquid Indian equities is, from a risk factor perspective, essentially one trade: long Indian economic growth, long FII appetite for emerging market risk.

Forty names give you the feeling of safety. The mathematics does not care about your feelings.

What Real Diversification Requires

True diversification requires separating your capital across genuinely different risk factors. In practice, for an Indian private investor, this means thinking across at least four dimensions.

First, asset class. Equity, fixed income, gold, and real estate do not move together through full cycles. Gold in particular has historically served Indian portfolios well as a crisis hedge — both the 2008 and 2020 corrections saw gold holding value or rising while equities collapsed. A portfolio with no allocation outside equities is not diversified; it is concentrated in one asset class with many names.

Second, geography. Indian markets are correlated with global sentiment but not with every global economy. An allocation to international equities — US index funds, for instance, now accessible through domestic feeder funds — provides genuine factor diversification. The Indian rupee's secular depreciation trend also makes international assets a partial currency hedge.

Third, time horizon and liquidity. Liquid assets, semi-liquid assets, and illiquid assets serve different roles in a portfolio. Forced selling of liquid assets during a correction is the mechanism by which short-term volatility becomes permanent capital loss. Having a portion of your portfolio in assets that simply cannot be sold — fixed deposits locked for three years, unlisted equity, long-duration bonds — removes the psychological and practical temptation to sell at the wrong time.

Fourth, business cycle sensitivity. Within equities, the distinction is not SEBI sector classification but earnings sensitivity to the economic cycle. Infrastructure and capital goods are highly cyclical. Consumer staples and healthcare are less so. Holding both is real within-equity diversification; holding Reliance, TCS, HDFC Bank, and Infosys is not — they are all large liquid names that will be sold simultaneously in a risk-off event.

The Case for Concentration

I should be honest about the counter-argument, because it is a real one. If you have genuine edge — deep knowledge of a sector, a business, a management team — concentration beats diversification. The best returns I have seen in my career came from clients who held four or five businesses they understood completely and held them for a decade. Diversification, after all, is protection against ignorance. If you are not ignorant about what you own, you need less of it.

But this requires a level of conviction and knowledge that most investors, including sophisticated ones, do not actually have. They think they have it. They have read the annual reports. They have spoken to the management. But they have not lived through a full credit cycle with that business, have not seen how management behaves when things go badly, have not watched what happens to earnings when a key client walks away. Real conviction is earned over years, not assembled from a research note.

For most investors, most of the time, the honest answer is: you do not have that edge. Build a portfolio that acknowledges that honestly. Diversify across risk factors, not just names. Accept that forty stocks that all fall together is a single position wearing a disguise.
