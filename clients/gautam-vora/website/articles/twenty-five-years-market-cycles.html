Twenty-Five Years of Indian Market Cycles: What I Know Now That I Didn't Then

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

I started in this business in the late 1990s, when the Indian technology sector was being discovered by global capital and a generation of first-time equity investors was learning what a P/E ratio was — not from textbooks but from watching stocks double and triple in months. It was an intoxicating environment to enter. It was also an excellent education in how markets behave at extremes, though I did not fully understand the lesson until several years later, after I had lived through what came next.

Four major dislocations in twenty-five years. Each one felt different in the moment. Each one looked, in hindsight, like a variation on the same underlying theme. I want to write about what I actually learned from each of them — not the sanitised version that sounds wise in retrospect, but what I got wrong, what I got right, and what the pattern across all four of them tells me about how markets actually work.

2001: The first lesson in narrative fragility

The dot-com bust of 2000–2001 was my first experience of a market turning violently against a consensus that had felt, for a sustained period, like settled truth. The narrative going into the peak was airtight: the internet changes everything, traditional valuation metrics do not apply to businesses with exponential user growth, India's technology sector is the beneficiary of a multi-decade global outsourcing shift. Every part of that narrative was, in some sense, correct. The outsourcing thesis played out over the next two decades exactly as its proponents claimed. The companies that survived created enormous wealth.

But the prices at the peak embedded not just the correct thesis but fifteen years of that thesis's realisation, discounted back to the present at multiples that left no margin for error, no allowance for the inevitable delays and setbacks. When sentiment turned, the correct thesis provided no protection at all. Stocks fell sixty, seventy, eighty percent — not because the underlying story was wrong, but because the price had run so far ahead of any reasonable version of the story that no amount of fundamental vindication could justify it in the near term.

"This was the first thing I learned: a correct thesis at the wrong price is still a bad investment. The market does not reward being right about the future. It rewards being right about the future when that future is not already in the price."

2008: The lesson about leverage

By 2008 I had been through one full cycle and thought I understood markets reasonably well. I understood them well enough to be worried about valuations in 2007. What I underestimated — what almost everyone underestimated — was the degree to which the global financial system had become leveraged against American housing prices, and how a problem that originated thousands of miles away could transmit itself with such violence to Indian equities, Indian liquidity, and Indian corporate balance sheets that had no direct exposure to subprime mortgages.

The Sensex fell nearly sixty percent from its January 2008 peak to its March 2009 trough. Good companies — companies with clean balance sheets, growing earnings, and genuine competitive moats — fell as much as mediocre ones. Liquidity did not discriminate. When global funds needed to raise cash, they sold what they could sell, not what they wanted to sell. I watched excellent businesses trade at five times earnings because the seller was a forced seller and there were no buyers.

What I learned from 2008 was about the distinction between price and value, applied under genuine duress. I had understood it intellectually before. In 2008 I understood it viscerally: a permanent loss of capital and a temporary decline in price are fundamentally different things, but they feel identical in the moment. The companies that emerged from 2008 with clean balance sheets and intact businesses went on to deliver extraordinary returns over the following decade. The ones that were over-leveraged — whether the company itself or the investor holding them on margin — did not get that opportunity.

2020: Speed and recovery

The COVID crash of March 2020 was unlike any previous crisis in one specific respect: its speed. The Sensex lost roughly forty percent of its value in approximately forty trading days. There was no gradual deterioration, no early warning period during which an attentive investor could reduce risk. By the time the news had turned genuinely alarming, much of the damage was already done.

"In March 2020 the narrative was that the global economy was shutting down indefinitely. Six months later the Sensex was back at pre-COVID levels. The crisis that felt most existential in the moment had the shortest recovery in my experience. That taught me something important about the relationship between narrative intensity and market duration."

What 2020 confirmed, for me, was that the size and fear-level of a crisis narrative does not predict recovery time. 2008 — a genuine structural crisis in the global financial system — took years to fully recover. 2020 — a crisis of equal or greater apparent severity in terms of media coverage and political response — recovered in months, because the underlying financial system was sound and the policy response was overwhelming. Trying to predict recovery timelines from narrative intensity is a fool's game. What matters is whether the underlying economic and financial structure is intact. In 2020 it was, even if it did not feel that way in March.

2022: The return of an old enemy

The 2022 correction — driven by the fastest global rate-hiking cycle in forty years — was in some ways the most intellectually interesting of the four, because it punished a specific type of investor rather than the market broadly. Companies with distant, speculative earnings — the high-multiple technology and new-age businesses that had attracted enormous capital during the zero-rate era — fell fifty, sixty, sometimes eighty percent. Companies with real earnings, real cash flows, and reasonable valuations held up considerably better. The market was, for once, punishing valuation excess with some precision.

The thread running through all four episodes is the same: narrative certainty peaks right before the reversal. In 2000 the consensus was that valuation multiples had permanently reset higher. In 2007 the consensus was that global liquidity would remain abundant indefinitely. In early 2020 the consensus was that the economy would not function normally for years. In 2021 the consensus was that rates would stay near zero for the foreseeable future. Each of these consensuses was held with great conviction by intelligent, experienced people at precisely the moment it was about to be proven wrong.

I do not know what the next crisis will be or when it will arrive. I do know that when I find myself feeling certain — really certain — about the direction of markets, I have learned to treat that certainty itself as a warning sign. The market's most reliable habit, across twenty-five years and four crises, is this: it reverses when the narrative feels most settled. That is not a timing tool. It is a disposition. And it is the most useful thing I know.
