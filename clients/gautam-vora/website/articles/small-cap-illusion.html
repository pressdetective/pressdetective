The Small-Cap Illusion: Why Most Small-Cap Funds Consistently Fail Their Investors

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

The pitch for small-cap investing is compelling and, at its core, correct. Small companies are under-researched. Markets are less efficient at pricing them. A skilled analyst who does the primary work — visits the factory, speaks to the customers, understands the competitive dynamics in a niche industry — can identify businesses worth significantly more than their current market price before the rest of the market catches on. This is a real and repeatable source of alpha. I have seen it work. The problem is that it only works at a scale that most fund managers are not willing to operate at, because limiting scale limits fee income.

What most retail investors buy when they invest in a small-cap fund is not this skill. They buy a large, diversified portfolio of 70 to 100 small-cap names, managed by a team that collectively owns the same names as every other small-cap fund in the country, at an AUM that makes genuine position management during market stress effectively impossible. The category is crowded. The advantage has been arbitraged away by the money flowing into it. And the structural features of small-cap investing mean the damage is asymmetric — the good years are good, but the bad years are worse than the category promises.

Three Structural Problems

The first problem is illiquidity-driven forced selling. Small-cap stocks, by definition, have lower trading volumes. A fund with Rs 15,000 crore in AUM trying to reduce its position in a small-cap stock with average daily volume of Rs 5 crore faces a mathematical impossibility. It cannot sell without moving the price against itself significantly. When redemptions come in during a market stress — which is always when small-cap markets are already falling and illiquid — the fund manager is forced to sell at precisely the wrong time and at prices that crystallise losses for investors who are not even redeeming. The redemption pressure of a minority of investors damages the holdings of everyone who stays. This is not a hypothetical. It happened visibly and painfully in the 2018-2019 small-cap correction and again in 2020.

The second problem is manager herding. Pull up the top-25 holdings of any ten Indian small-cap funds and count the names that appear in more than five of them. The overlap is remarkable. This happens because small-cap research capacity at Indian fund houses is genuinely limited, because the quality small-cap businesses that meet institutional criteria are a relatively small universe, and because no fund manager wants to own names that their benchmark doesn't contain and that their peers don't own — career risk is minimised by being wrong in good company. The result is that the undiscovered-gem narrative of small-cap investing conceals a portfolio that looks, in aggregate, like a concentrated bet on the same fifty names as everyone else.

The third problem is the mathematics of AUM growth. Small-cap alpha is a function of operating below the threshold at which your own activity affects prices. A Rs 500 crore small-cap fund can build and exit positions without meaningfully moving markets. A Rs 20,000 crore fund is the market in the stocks it owns. The same manager, running the same strategy, with the same research capability, generates structurally lower alpha as AUM grows — because they must either expand into lower-quality names to deploy capital, or accept that their top ideas are now crowded by their own ownership. Most successful small-cap managers face this problem and most resolve it by continuing to grow AUM, because that maximises their management company's economics, even as it destroys the investor's return proposition.

The One Thing Good Managers Do Differently

The exceptions — the small-cap managers who have genuinely delivered risk-adjusted outperformance over long periods — share one characteristic: they have limited AUM, either by choice or by the constraints of how their fund is structured. In India, this means looking for funds that have soft-closed or hard-closed to new investors at a scale that preserves the strategy's integrity. These funds are harder to get into. They do not advertise aggressively because they do not want new money. Their distributors do not push them because there is no distribution incentive on a closed fund.

When evaluating any small-cap fund, the questions that matter are: what is the AUM relative to the investable universe of the strategy? What is the average daily liquidity of the portfolio as a percentage of AUM? Has the fund ever capped new inflows, and if not, why not? How many names are in the portfolio, and how many of those names also appear in the top holdings of the five largest competing funds? A fund that fails these tests may still deliver returns in a bull market. It will fail its investors in the correction. And in small-cap markets, the correction always comes.
