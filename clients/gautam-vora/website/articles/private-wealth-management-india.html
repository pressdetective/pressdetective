What the Private Wealth Management Industry in India Won't Tell You

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

I have been in this industry for over twenty-five years. I have sat across the table from wealth managers at private banks, brokerages, and independent advisory firms. I have seen how they are trained, what their incentive structures look like, and what they actually do when a client is not watching. Let me tell you something that the industry's glossy brochures will never admit: the conflict of interest in Indian wealth management is not an occasional problem. It is structural. It is baked in. And most clients have no idea it exists.

The product quota problem

Here is how most large wealth management operations work in India. A relationship manager sits in a branch or a private banking suite. He has a book of clients — families with assets between, say, two crore and fifty crore. He is paid a base salary. But a meaningful chunk of his total compensation comes from product distribution — commissions on insurance products, trail fees on mutual fund AUM, distributor margins on structured products and portfolio management schemes. His quarterly performance review includes targets: how much new SIP book did you add, how many insurance policies did you sell, how much did you move into the in-house PMS?

That relationship manager may genuinely like his clients. He may be a decent person. But every time he sits across a client and recommends a product, there is an invisible third party in the room: his employer's product shelf. And the products that pay the highest commissions are rarely the products best suited to the client's needs.

"The question you should always ask your wealth manager is: are you paid by me, or by the products you sell me? If the answer is the latter, you do not have an advisor. You have a distributor."

The language of alignment

The industry has become sophisticated in disguising this conflict. Relationship managers no longer say "I recommend this product." They say "based on your risk profile, this solution is appropriate for your goals." The word "solution" does the work that "product" used to do, but it sounds more personalised, more considered. Risk profiling questionnaires — which typically take about four minutes to complete — provide a thin veneer of process. The outcome, in the majority of cases, is the same: a portfolio that reflects the distributor's margin economics more than the client's genuine requirements.

Fee-based advisory — where the advisor is paid a flat annual fee or a percentage of AUM by the client, with no product commissions — exists in India, but it is still a small segment. SEBI introduced the Registered Investment Adviser framework precisely to create a cleaner category of fiduciary advisors. The uptake has been slow. Most HNI clients still work with bank-linked relationship managers or distributor-model advisors, often without realising what category their "advisor" actually falls into.

What genuine advice looks like

A real advisor — one who is structurally capable of acting in your interest — has several distinguishing features. First, the fee structure is transparent and paid by you, not by the product manufacturer. Second, your assets are held in independent custody: with a depository, a registrar, or a custodian that has no relationship with the advisor's firm. Third — and this is the one most people overlook — a genuine advisor is willing to tell you to do nothing.

The bias toward action is pervasive in this industry because action generates fees and commissions. When markets fall, a good advisor's job is often to talk a client out of doing anything. When markets rally and the temptation is to add exposure, the right answer is sometimes to rebalance toward safer assets. Neither of those recommendations generates revenue for a commission-based advisor. Both are essential services for the client.

  • Ask whether your advisor holds an SEBI RIA registration — this imposes a fiduciary standard that distributors do not face.
  • Ask for a complete fee disclosure, including all trail commissions and distribution margins earned on your portfolio.
  • Ask who holds your assets in custody — it should not be the same entity advising you on what to buy.
  • Ask your advisor what they recommended clients do in March 2020, in January 2022, and in October 2023. The answers will tell you more than any marketing presentation.
"The best advisory relationship I have seen is one where the client calls the advisor in a panic and the advisor's response is: 'Nothing has changed about your plan. Please do not do anything.' That takes integrity. It also takes a fee structure that does not reward action."

India's wealth management industry will mature. It always does, and SEBI is pushing in the right direction. But that process will take years. In the meantime, the most valuable thing you can do as a client is understand exactly how the person managing your money is compensated — and by whom. That single piece of knowledge changes every conversation you will ever have with them.
