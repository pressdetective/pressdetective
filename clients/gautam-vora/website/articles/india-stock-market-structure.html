Understanding India's Stock Exchange Structure: NSE, BSE and SEBI Regulation

By Gautam Vora  |  Equity Analyst, Mumbai  |  June 2026
← Back to Commentary

India operates two primary stock exchanges — the National Stock Exchange (NSE) and the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) — alongside a regulatory framework run by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI). For anyone serious about investing in Indian equities, understanding how these institutions interact is foundational.

The NSE and BSE: Different Origins, Shared Purpose

The Bombay Stock Exchange, established in 1875, is Asia's oldest stock exchange. For over a century it operated as a floor-traded, broker-dominated exchange. The National Stock Exchange, formed in 1992 at the recommendation of the Pherwani Committee, was designed explicitly to break that model — it introduced anonymous electronic trading, transparent order matching, and a dematerialised settlement system.

NSE's launch of screen-based trading in 1994 was transformative. Within a few years it overtook BSE in traded volume, and today NSE accounts for the majority of equity and nearly all derivatives turnover. Both exchanges list most large-cap companies, so an investor buying a Nifty constituent can do so on either. The practical difference shows up in mid and small-caps, where NSE typically offers tighter spreads and deeper liquidity.

SEBI: The Regulatory Backbone

The Securities and Exchange Board of India was established in 1988 and given statutory authority through the SEBI Act of 1992. Its mandate covers every participant in the securities market:

  • Listed companies and their continuous disclosure obligations
  • Brokers, sub-brokers, depository participants and other intermediaries
  • Mutual funds, alternative investment funds, and portfolio managers
  • Takeover and acquisition processes under the Takeover Code
  • Insider trading prevention and market surveillance

For investors, SEBI's most consequential function is maintaining market integrity. The introduction of T+1 settlement (phased in 2022–23) placed India among the world's fastest-settling equity markets — ahead of most developed markets still on T+2.

Key data point: India's market capitalisation crossed $4 trillion in 2024, making it the fourth-largest equity market globally. SEBI's ongoing reforms — from algo-trading regulation to ESG disclosure requirements — reflect a market that has matured significantly since liberalisation.

Why Exchange Structure Matters for Equity Analysis

The dual-exchange structure has practical implications for research and trading. NSE's derivatives market is where institutional positioning lives: open interest data, PCR (put-call ratio) trends, and implied volatility surfaces all provide signals that pure cash market data misses. Any serious equity analyst in India cross-references cash market action against F&O data routinely.

SEBI's mandatory quarterly shareholding disclosures are among the most valuable public datasets available. They reveal promoter stake changes, FII and DII inflows, and pledge changes — all before they fully reflect in price. Understanding how to read shareholding patterns is as important as reading a P&L.

The Road Ahead

SEBI's regulatory agenda has expanded into new territory: social media disclosure obligations for listed companies, stricter algorithmic trading guardrails, and sustainability reporting aligned with BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) standards. For an equity analyst based in Mumbai, staying current with SEBI circulars is as important as tracking quarterly earnings. The market rewards those who understand the regulatory terrain as well as the financial statements.
