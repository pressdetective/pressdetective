FIIs, SIPs and What Actually Moves Indian Markets Now

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

For the first twenty years of my career, the question every serious market participant asked at the start of the week was some version of: what are the FIIs doing? Foreign institutional flows were the tide that lifted and sank all boats. When FIIs bought, markets went up. When they sold, markets went down. The domestic institutional presence was too small, the retail investor base too thin and too easily spooked, to provide any meaningful counterweight. A few billion dollars of FII selling could move the Sensex 5% in a week and there was nothing domestic capital could do about it.

That world no longer exists. The structural shift in Indian market dynamics over the past eight years is, in my view, the single most important change in how Indian equities actually function. And yet I still encounter investors and commentators who interpret every sharp correction as an FII-driven warning signal about India's fundamentals, when in most cases what they are actually seeing is an opportunity created by a temporary imbalance that domestic capital will correct.

The New Architecture of Indian Markets

Monthly SIP inflows crossed Rs 20,000 crore in 2024 and have maintained above that level consistently. To put this in perspective: that is roughly $2.5 billion per month flowing into Indian equities through the systematic investment plan route alone, before any lump-sum purchases, before direct equity investment, and before the significant flows from insurance companies, EPF, and other domestic institutions. The aggregate domestic institutional purchase figure in recent months has regularly exceeded FII net selling by a substantial margin.

The consequence is structural. Domestic money does not have a risk-off button in the same way FII money does. When a global event triggers FII selling — a US Federal Reserve surprise, a geopolitical shock, a credit event in another emerging market — FII desks in Mumbai execute redemption orders from investors who may be in London, New York, or Singapore and whose decision to sell Indian equities is driven by factors entirely unrelated to India's economic trajectory. The domestic SIP investor, by contrast, is by design investing a fixed amount every month regardless of market conditions. Their money comes in during the correction, not out.

Sharp corrections are increasingly buying opportunities for domestic investors, not signals of structural trouble. The FII sell-off creates the discount; domestic flows absorb it.

What This Means for Volatility and Sector Leadership

Indian market volatility has structurally decreased relative to comparable emerging markets, and I believe this is primarily attributable to the domestic flow floor. The 2022 correction, which in a previous era would have been a prolonged bear market driven by FII exit, was arrested and reversed within a matter of months as domestic institutional money absorbed the FII selling at lower prices. The same pattern was visible in the correction of late 2024.

Sector leadership has also shifted in ways that directly reflect the composition of domestic investor money. The SIP investor's money goes predominantly into large-cap and flexicap funds, but the growing prevalence of small and mid-cap fund allocations in retail portfolios has created sustained demand for quality mid-cap businesses that simply did not exist fifteen years ago. This explains, at least partially, why mid-caps have outperformed large-caps over the past three-year period — there is genuine new structural buying in that segment that the market has been working to price.

The second major sectoral consequence is in financials. The bank and NBFC sector has been the primary recipient of domestic retail investment flows, both through equity funds and through direct SIP mandates into financial sector funds. This has maintained valuations in financials at levels that purely FII-driven analysis might consider stretched, and has made the sector resilient to the kind of valuation compression that FII-led corrections used to produce.

The Practical Implication

The practical implication for how to position a portfolio in this environment is this: do not interpret FII selling as a structural signal about India. Interpret it as a technical event — a temporary excess of supply over demand that the domestic flow machine will absorb. The pace at which it absorbs depends on the severity of the global risk-off event. If it is a genuine global financial crisis, the absorption takes longer. If it is a routine emerging-market rotation, the absorption happens within weeks.

In either case, the appropriate response for a long-term domestic investor is to treat sharp corrections as the buying windows they have historically been in this new market structure, rather than as reasons to reassess India's medium-term story. The FII tail no longer wags the Indian dog. This is new. It matters enormously. And most commentary on Indian markets has not fully adjusted to it yet.
