How I Value Indian Financial Companies: A Framework After 25 Years

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

Financial companies are the hardest businesses to value in India. They are also among the most important, given the sector's weight in both the Sensex and in domestic portfolio allocations. I have watched investors repeatedly cycle through the same mistake: buying an Indian bank or NBFC because it looks cheap on price-to-book, holding it for three years while the stock does nothing or falls further, and then discovering that what looked like a value opportunity was actually a balance sheet problem that the reported numbers had not yet fully reflected.

P/B is not a valuation framework. It is a starting point for a conversation. The conversation is about what the book is actually worth, which is an entirely separate question from what management reports it to be. Let me describe how I actually think about financial companies after twenty-five years of getting some of these right and getting some of them badly wrong.

Loan Book Quality: The NPA Recognition Culture

The single most important variable in valuing an Indian financial company is the quality and honesty of its NPA recognition. This is not the same as the reported NPA number. A low reported NPA can mean: (a) the loan book is genuinely clean; or (b) the management is classifying stressed assets in ways that delay recognition. Distinguishing between these two interpretations requires looking at proxies rather than the reported headline.

The proxies I look at: the divergence between RBI inspection findings and reported NPAs in previous audit cycles — any financial that has repeatedly shown large divergences between what RBI found and what management reported is telling you something important about recognition culture. The pace of GNPA recognition relative to peers during economic stress — a bank that reports stable NPAs while the sector is seeing stress is either genuinely better or is delaying. The track record over a full credit cycle — how did this specific management team handle the 2018 NBFC crisis or the 2020 COVID shock? Did they front-load recognition and provision aggressively, or did they drip-feed it over multiple quarters? Front-loaders are the ones worth owning.

A low reported NPA can mean the book is clean, or it can mean management is delaying recognition. Distinguishing between these two requires looking at proxies, not headlines.

Deposit Franchise: The Cost of Funds Advantage

In Indian banking, the cost of funds is a structural moat when it exists, and a structural vulnerability when it doesn't. A bank with a high CASA ratio — current account and savings account deposits as a proportion of total deposits — funds itself cheaply and durably. CASA depositors are sticky; they are not repricing with every RBI rate cycle. A bank dependent on bulk deposits or certificate of deposit funding is exposed to funding cost volatility that directly compresses margins when rates rise.

The CASA ratio is widely reported, but the quality question underneath it is: why do these depositors keep their money here? Is it genuine franchise strength — branch network, customer relationships, salary account mandates from employers — or is it an artifact of a particular geography or employer relationship that could shift? The best franchise banks in India have built CASA bases that have held up through rate cycles and competitive pressure. The weakest have CASA numbers that look reasonable until a competitor offers 50 basis points more on savings accounts and the base moves.

Management Through a Full Credit Cycle

Financial companies are the businesses where management quality matters most and is hardest to assess in normal times. In benign credit environments, almost all managements look good. Loan growth is strong, NPAs are low, NIMs are healthy, and the only question is which financial is growing fastest. The differentiation happens in downturns.

I insist on having a view of how the current management team behaved during at least one period of significant credit stress. Did they communicate transparently with investors about deterioration? Did they move early to provision, accepting short-term earnings pain to protect long-term book quality? Did they manage capital conservatively or did they continue to grow the book through the stress? Did they raise equity when they needed it, or delay until it became unavoidably necessary at punishing valuations?

Managements that handled the 2018-2019 period well and the 2020 period well have demonstrated something real. Managements whose track record only covers the 2021-2025 bull run haven't been tested.

The Value Trap Question

A financial company is a value trap when the reported book value overstates the economic book value — when there are losses embedded in the loan portfolio that have not yet flowed through the P&L. The trap springs when those losses are eventually recognised, book value falls, and the investor discovers they were paying 0.8x book for a business whose real book was 0.6x what was reported.

The test: is the provisioning coverage ratio adequate given the stress indicators in the book? Is the management actively reducing exposure to the sectors where stress is building? Is the capital adequacy buffer comfortable enough that recognition of embedded losses will not require dilutive equity issuance? A financial that passes these tests at 0.8x book is genuinely cheap. One that fails them at 0.5x book is a trap.

The framework is not complicated. The discipline required to apply it consistently, rather than reaching for the apparently low valuation, is.
