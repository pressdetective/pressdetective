What Warren Buffett Got Right — And What Does Not Apply to India

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

Every serious investor in India has read Buffett. Many have studied him extensively. Some have built investment frameworks that are essentially translations of his approach into Indian terminology. I have spent twenty-five years watching what happens when those frameworks meet actual Indian companies, and I want to be precise about where Buffett's thinking is genuinely illuminating and where it leads Indian investors into a specific and repeated category of mistake.

Let me start with what he got right, because the principles are correct and they are worth stating clearly: buy businesses you understand deeply, at prices below their intrinsic value; hold them for long periods; do not confuse activity with progress; treat market price fluctuations as an opportunity rather than a signal. These are principles of rationality in the face of markets that systematically overprice short-term visibility and underprice long-term business quality. They apply in India. They apply everywhere. They are evergreen.

Where the Translation Breaks Down

The problems begin when Indian investors apply not Buffett's principles but his specific frameworks — the checklists, the sector preferences, the structural features he looks for — to Indian businesses without asking whether those features mean the same thing here.

The consumer brand moat is the clearest example. Buffett loves consumer brands because in developed markets, decades of advertising investment create genuine switching costs in the consumer's mind. The brand is a moat because consumer behaviour in those markets is relatively stable and brand loyalty, once established, is durable. In India, this is partially true and increasingly qualified. Indian consumers at every income level are demonstrably more willing to switch to better value or better quality than equivalent consumers in the US or UK. The e-commerce infrastructure that now reaches deep into tier-2 and tier-3 cities has accelerated this. A brand moat in Indian FMCG is real but it is meaningfully narrower and more easily eroded than its US equivalent.

The insurance float model is another example. Buffett built Berkshire around the idea that insurance float — premium income held before claims are paid — provides essentially free or negative-cost capital for investment. This is a genuine structural advantage in mature, disciplined insurance markets. The Indian insurance sector is growing rapidly and the long-term opportunity is real. But the underwriting culture and claims management discipline that makes float genuinely valuable in the Berkshire model takes decades to develop. Buying Indian insurance companies on the logic of "this is what Buffett would do" without examining the actual underwriting quality of that specific company is a category error.

The question Buffett asks about management is: are they honest and competent? In India, the question has a third dimension: what do they do with cash that doesn't belong to them?

The Variable Buffett Cannot Price from Omaha

The most important limitation of applying Buffett's framework to India is the one that is hardest to quantify: the promoter.

In the US, public companies are predominantly managed by professional executives with minority ownership stakes. The relevant management questions are about competence and integrity in the conventional sense. Buffett's approach to evaluating management — do they treat shareholders like partners, do they communicate honestly about mistakes, do they avoid empire-building acquisitions — is calibrated for this context.

In India, the majority of listed companies are promoter-led, with founding families holding 40-70% stakes. The relevant management question is different. It is not just: are they honest and competent? It is: what do they do with capital that is technically the company's but over which they exercise effective personal control? The range of answers to that question in Indian markets is wider than anything Buffett's framework was designed to assess. At one end, promoters who treat minority shareholders as genuine partners and have compounded capital for decades at extraordinary rates. At the other end, promoters who have systematically extracted value through related-party transactions, pledged their stakes for personal leverage, or diverted cash flows in ways that technically comply with disclosure requirements while comprehensively failing the spirit of shareholder protection.

The India-specific skill in equity investing is the ability to assess promoter character and capital allocation behaviour across a full business cycle. This is not in any of the books. It is learned through observation, through failure, and through the particular kind of attention that comes from watching the same people manage money — their shareholders' and their own — through multiple phases of the same cycle. Buffett understood this about management in general. In India, it is not a general principle. It is the central variable.
