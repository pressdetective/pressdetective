Mumbai: What India's Financial Capital Looks Like from the Inside

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

I started my career in the mid-1990s at a brokerage firm in a narrow lane off Dalal Street where the telephone cables ran along the outside of the building because nobody had bothered to route them properly inside. There were traders who kept handwritten ledgers. There were runners — actual human beings — whose job was to carry paper between offices. The BSE trading ring was still in use. You could hear the noise of it in the corridor.

That world is gone. But the city that contained it is still the same city, and after twenty-five years working here, I think the things that make Mumbai genuinely important to Indian finance are not the ones that appear in the brochures.

Three Eras, One City

Dalal Street in the 1990s was a world of relationships built over decades, information asymmetries that were frankly enormous, and a culture in which who you knew mattered more than what you knew. Harshad Mehta was the extreme expression of a system that rewarded access and aggression over analysis. When the scam broke, the shock was real — but so was the underlying truth it revealed about how capital had been moving through that system.

Nariman Point in the 2000s was something different: the arrival of the multinational financial machine. Investment banks, FII desks, equity research that was being read in London and New York as well as in Mumbai. The Bloomberg terminal became the status symbol of that era. The implicit assumption was that professionalism meant convergence — that Indian markets would come to function like developed markets, that the old relationship networks would be replaced by transparent, analysis-driven capital allocation. This was partially true and mostly wrong.

BKC today is the third era: private wealth management, family offices, domestic institutional money, fintech. The money has grown enormously. Mutual fund AUM that was a rounding error in 2005 now rivals FII holdings in aggregate. The SIP investor in Pune or Ahmedabad who has never visited Mumbai is now a structural force in Indian markets. The city's financial gravity has shifted outward while the physical infrastructure has shifted to the reclaimed land of the Bandra-Kurla Complex.

The Bloomberg terminal became the status symbol of that era. The implicit assumption was that Indian markets would converge toward developed markets. This was partially true and mostly wrong.

What the Research Reports Miss

Here is what I have learned that does not appear in any equity research note. The informal intelligence network in Mumbai finance is still the most important source of real information about Indian businesses. Not illegal information — I am not talking about price-sensitive material. I mean the accumulated judgment of people who have watched the same promoters, the same sectors, and the same cycles for twenty or thirty years.

When a mid-cap company's CFO resigns unexpectedly, the analysts at the banks write a note analyzing the stated reasons. The people who have been watching that promoter for fifteen years know in forty-eight hours whether this is significant or not — not because they have inside information, but because they have pattern recognition that no model can replicate. That knowledge lives in conversations over dinner in Colaba, in WhatsApp groups between former colleagues, in the shared memory of people who were all in this market together through 2001, 2008, 2013, 2020.

Mumbai is still the place where that knowledge accumulates. Bengaluru has more technology; Delhi has more political access; Ahmedabad and Chennai have deep industry networks. But the intersection of equity markets, debt markets, private capital, regulatory relationships, and media — that intersection still happens here, in this peculiar city of reclaimed land and monsoon traffic and extraordinary professional density.

What the City Has Taught Me

Twenty-five years of conversations in Mumbai have taught me three things about how capital actually flows in India. First, trust moves faster than information. A deal that looks puzzling on paper often makes sense when you understand the relationship structure behind it. Second, the promoter is the variable that the models cannot price. The same business, with the same balance sheet and the same market position, is worth dramatically different amounts depending on who controls it and what they do with cash. Third, cycles in India are longer and deeper than people expect on the way up, and shorter and shallower than people fear on the way down.

None of this is on a Bloomberg terminal. All of it is available if you spend enough time in this city, talking to the right people, and paying attention.
