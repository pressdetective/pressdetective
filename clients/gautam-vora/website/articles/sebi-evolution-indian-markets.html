SEBI at 35: How Indian Markets Finally Grew Up

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

I entered this industry in the late 1990s, just a few years after SEBI had been given statutory teeth following the Harshad Mehta scandal. The market I entered was still largely a broker's market — opaque, relationship-driven, rife with price manipulation and front-running, and largely inaccessible to ordinary retail investors in any meaningful sense. Settlement took weeks. Price rigging was commonplace. The retail investor was, in practice, the last to know anything and the first to lose money.

Thirty-five years on, India has one of the most sophisticated equity market infrastructures in the world. That is not a claim I make lightly — I have seen the system at close range through every phase of its development. The transformation is real, and SEBI deserves substantial credit for it. But the job is not finished, and acknowledging progress should not mean ignoring the gaps that remain.

The reforms that genuinely changed the game

T+1 settlement is the reform I am most proud to have witnessed. India moved to T+1 — trade settlement in one business day — for all equity securities in January 2023, becoming the first major market in the world to do so at scale. The US, despite the resources of Wall Street, only reached T+1 in 2024 after years of debate. India got there first and implemented it cleanly. The capital efficiency benefit for the entire market — reduced margin requirements, lower systemic risk, faster access to sale proceeds — is substantial and largely underappreciated in domestic commentary.

The mutual fund expense ratio caps, progressively tightened since 2018, represent SEBI's most direct intervention in investor costs. Before the reforms, TER (Total Expense Ratio) compression was entirely voluntary and largely absent. Post-reform, the industry has been forced to pass on scale benefits to investors. Index fund costs in India are now genuinely competitive globally. The shift toward low-cost products has been material.

"The move to T+1 settlement was not an incremental upgrade. It was a structural leap that put India ahead of every major developed market. It happened because the regulator pushed when the industry resisted."

The insider trading framework has been comprehensively overhauled — twice. The 2015 regulations introduced the concept of a Connected Person, broadened the definition of unpublished price sensitive information, and made the compliance architecture significantly more robust. Enforcement has not been perfect, but the number of high-profile insider trading cases prosecuted in the past decade dwarfs the previous two decades combined. The message that the regulator is watching has been received.

On market access, the introduction of the Qualified Foreign Investor and later the Foreign Portfolio Investor categories rationalised a structure that had been needlessly fragmented. The ASBA mechanism — where application money for IPOs stays in the investor's account until allotment — eliminated the float abuse that used to be standard practice. The UPI-based IPO application system extended that protection to retail investors without demat accounts at large banks. These are unglamorous plumbing changes that collectively made the market more honest.

What still needs work

The F&O retail protection problem is the most urgent unfinished business. SEBI's own research — published in 2023 and updated in 2024 — showed that roughly nine in ten individual F&O traders lose money, and that aggregate losses to individual traders run to tens of thousands of crore rupees annually. The exchange and broker ecosystem profits handsomely from this activity. SEBI has taken some steps — increasing contract sizes, tightening margin requirements, restricting weekly expiry proliferation — but the fundamental issue remains: a product designed for institutional risk management is being actively marketed to retail participants who lack the capital, sophistication, and risk management infrastructure to use it safely.

Related-party disclosure quality is the second gap. SEBI mandated RPT disclosures years ago, but the quality and granularity of those disclosures varies enormously. A promoter-controlled company can technically comply with the disclosure requirement while still obscuring the economic substance of transactions through aggregation and boilerplate language. The gap between what is disclosed and what a sophisticated analyst can actually understand is still too wide.

"SEBI has built a market infrastructure that competes with the best in the world. The next frontier is not market structure — it is ensuring that the millions of new retail participants who have entered since 2020 are protected from products and practices designed to extract from them rather than serve them."

None of this diminishes the achievement. A regulator that inherited a market shattered by a securities scam and delivered, three and a half decades later, a globally competitive exchange infrastructure with genuine institutional depth — that is a serious institutional success. The question now is whether SEBI can apply the same rigour to investor protection that it applied to market structure. The next generation of Indian equity investors is watching.
