What I Have Learned from 25 Years of Managing Private Client Wealth

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

The most important conversation I ever had with a client lasted forty minutes and had nothing to do with markets. He had come in for a portfolio review. His investments were performing well. But about twenty minutes into the meeting, something changed in his posture, and he said: "I should tell you something." What followed was a description of a family dispute over his late father's business assets — a dispute that had been running for three years, that involved a significant sum of money frozen in litigation, and that he had never mentioned to me because, he said, he "didn't think it was relevant to investing."

Everything about how I had structured his portfolio was wrong. Not because the underlying investments were poor — they weren't — but because I had been managing a portfolio for a life that didn't exist. His actual liquidity needs, his actual risk capacity, his actual time horizon were all different from what I had assumed. We spent the next hour rebuilding the strategy from scratch around his real situation. That was fifteen years ago. The restructured portfolio served him far better through everything that came after.

The Information Problem in Wealth Management

The wealth management industry has a fundamental structural problem: clients present the version of their finances they are comfortable with, and advisers build portfolios for that version. The result is portfolios optimised for an aspirational financial life rather than an actual one.

This happens for understandable reasons. Discussing a family business that is struggling with cashflow feels like admitting failure. Mentioning that the spouse does not agree with the investment approach feels like exposing a private disagreement. Disclosing an expected inheritance — or the complications around one — feels morbid or premature. So clients don't mention these things, and advisers don't press because pressing feels intrusive, and everyone proceeds on the basis of incomplete information.

The consequence is predictable. When the business hits a rough patch and the client needs to draw down the portfolio at exactly the wrong moment in the market cycle, the portfolio wasn't built to handle that. When the spouse's risk tolerance turns out to be far lower than the client's, the first sharp correction produces a crisis in the relationship as well as in the portfolio. When an inheritance arrives with conditions or complications attached, the asset allocation that made sense before doesn't make sense anymore.

Your job as a wealth manager is to understand the person, not just the portfolio. The portfolio is a consequence of the life, not the other way around.

What Trust Actually Enables

When clients trust you enough to share their real financial situation, three things become possible that weren't before.

First, genuine liquidity planning. I have seen portfolios destroyed not by bad investment choices but by forced selling at the wrong time — a business needed capital, or a property transaction fell through, or a family obligation arose that the client had known about for years but not mentioned. When I know about these obligations in advance, I can ensure there is always enough in genuinely liquid, low-volatility instruments that no market correction forces a sale of the equity portfolio. Liquidity risk is the silent killer of otherwise well-constructed portfolios.

Second, real risk calibration. Risk tolerance questionnaires are useful fictions. They tell you how a client thinks they will feel about a 20% drawdown in a hypothetical future. They tell you nothing about how they will actually feel when it happens. The only way to calibrate real risk tolerance is to understand what else is happening in the client's financial life — the obligations, the dependencies, the psychological relationship with money that often has roots in family history and life experience that no questionnaire will surface.

Third, honest goal-setting. Many clients have goals they have never stated explicitly because they seem too distant, too large, or too personal to bring into a financial meeting. When the relationship allows for that honesty, the portfolio can actually be pointed at the real destination rather than a proxy for it.

The Lesson I Keep Learning

After twenty-five years, the lesson I keep relearning is that the best investment outcome I can deliver to a client is rarely a function of which stocks I picked or which funds I recommended. It is a function of whether the portfolio survived the moments when the client's life put pressure on it — the divorce, the business crisis, the health emergency, the sudden need to support an ageing parent. The portfolios that survived those moments were the ones built with full knowledge of what was actually going on. The ones that didn't survive were the ones built on the presentable version of the client's finances.

Your job as a wealth manager is to understand the person, not just the portfolio. The portfolio is a consequence of the life, not the other way around.
