Why Indian Retail Investors Keep Losing Money — And What Actually Fixes It

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

People ask me all the time what they need to learn to become a better investor. More financial modelling? A course on derivatives? How to read a balance sheet properly? My answer disappoints them: none of that. The knowledge gap is not what is destroying retail investor returns in India. The behaviour gap is. And behaviour cannot be fixed with more information. It requires a different kind of self-awareness — one that most investors never develop because it is uncomfortable and because the financial media actively works against it.

Over twenty-five years I have watched the same patterns play out repeatedly, across bull markets and bear markets, across different client profiles and income levels. The losses cluster around three specific behavioural traps. Identify them in yourself and you will eliminate the vast majority of self-inflicted damage. Fail to identify them and no amount of market knowledge will save you.

Trap one: recency bias

Recency bias is the tendency to assume that what has happened recently will continue to happen. In equity markets this manifests as one of the most reliable wealth-destroyers known to finance: buying after a prolonged rally because the recent past makes continued gains feel inevitable, and selling after a prolonged decline because the recent past makes further losses feel certain.

The Indian market has provided vivid examples. In late 2021 and early 2022, retail SIP inflows were surging, demat account openings were at records, and every second conversation at a dinner party was about which stocks had doubled. The market had delivered extraordinary returns for eighteen months. New investors were pouring in, extrapolating that trajectory forward. The Sensex subsequently corrected roughly fifteen percent through 2022 as global rate hikes reset valuations — and many of those new entrants, having bought near the top on the strength of recent performance, experienced their first taste of losses exactly when their conviction was lowest.

"The time when investing feels most exciting and most obviously correct is usually the time when the forward return is lowest. The time when it feels most frightening is usually when the forward return is highest. Recency bias systematically inverts your instincts."

The fix is not to be a contrarian for its own sake. It is to decouple your allocation decisions from recent price performance and anchor them instead to valuation and your own financial plan. A market that has risen forty percent in two years is not a reason to invest more. It is a reason to check whether valuations still make sense at current prices.

Trap two: loss aversion

Losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. This asymmetry, documented extensively in behavioural economics, produces a specific and costly pattern in investment portfolios: investors sell their winners too early (locking in a pleasant gain before it can reverse) and hold their losers too long (refusing to realise a loss and admit they were wrong). The result is a portfolio that gradually concentrates in underperformers while the compounders leave the portfolio at the first sign of profit.

I have had clients hold a stock at a forty percent loss for three years because selling would mean "crystallising" the loss — as if the loss was not already real. The money was gone. The only question was whether to redeploy the remaining capital into something better or continue holding as a monument to a bad decision. Loss aversion makes that choice irrationally difficult. The psychological cost of admitting the mistake outweighs the financial logic of moving on.

Trap three: action bias

This is the one I encounter most often in private client conversations. When markets fall sharply — when the Sensex drops three percent in a day, when a global macro event dominates the news — clients call wanting to do something. Sell something. Hedge something. Rotate into something. The compulsion to act in a crisis is almost universal. It feels irresponsible to sit still. It feels like professional negligence, like intellectual passivity.

But in most cases, for a long-term equity investor with a properly constructed portfolio, the correct response to a market correction is nothing. Absolutely nothing. The portfolio was built to absorb exactly this kind of event. The businesses inside it have not changed. The thesis has not changed. The only thing that has changed is the price, and the price is temporary.

"When a client calls me in a panic asking what we should do, the most valuable thing I can say is: nothing. That answer is also the hardest to deliver, because it sounds like inaction rather than advice. But doing nothing is often the highest-conviction call you can make."

What I actually say to clients who cannot sit still is this: write down, right now, exactly what has changed about the businesses you own. Not the price — the businesses. If you cannot identify a specific, material change in the underlying business quality or the investment thesis, then you are reacting to noise. Go for a walk. Call me tomorrow. The urge to act will have faded, the businesses will be unchanged, and the portfolio will be fine.

The honest truth is that most retail investors would dramatically improve their outcomes by simply reducing their transaction frequency by half. Not by learning more. Not by finding better stocks. Just by doing less. The market does not reward activity. It rewards patience, consistency, and the discipline to let compounding work without interrupting it. Those are qualities of character, not qualities of knowledge. They cannot be downloaded. They have to be chosen, deliberately, in the moments when every instinct tells you to do the opposite.
