The Case for Unlisted Indian Companies: Where Real Wealth Is Being Created

By Gautam Vora  •  June 2026  •  Mumbai

← All articles

Consider how much wealth creation you have missed by looking only at listed markets. HDFC Bank's listing in 1995 was a remarkable opportunity. But the family that owned a significant stake before the listing made multiples of what even the earliest public market investors made. The same is true of dozens of businesses across India over the past three decades — the wealth creation happened in the years before the IPO, in the years when the company was invisible to anyone who only reads the economic newspapers.

This is not a historical observation. It describes what is happening right now in India's private sector. Some of the country's most interesting businesses — regional financial companies, specialty manufacturers, B2B technology service firms, healthcare businesses in tier-2 and tier-3 cities — are still unlisted. They are growing fast. They are generating real cash flows. And most of the investing public has no exposure to them at all.

The Information Asymmetry Is Extreme — and Legal

When I say information asymmetry, I am not talking about insider information. I mean the legitimate, entirely legal advantage that comes from doing primary research on a business that has no analyst coverage, no quarterly earnings call, and no market price to anchor your judgment. Listed markets are, in aggregate, reasonably efficient at processing publicly available information. Unlisted markets are not, because there is no mechanism through which information becomes reflexively priced.

A listed mid-cap company with twenty analysts covering it has a share price that reflects the consensus view of those twenty analysts plus the wisdom or otherwise of the thousands of market participants who read their work. An unlisted company of comparable quality has a valuation that reflects one thing only: what the last buyer and seller agreed on, which may have been two years ago and under circumstances unrelated to the company's current trajectory.

This creates genuine pricing anomalies. I have seen unlisted companies trading at 6x earnings while comparable listed peers traded at 25x, simply because the unlisted company had no institutional shareholder base pressing for a market-benchmarked valuation. The gap closes eventually — through an IPO, a private equity transaction, or a strategic sale. When it closes, the return profile is entirely different from what listed equity can offer.

The wealth creation happened in the years before the IPO, in the years when the company was invisible to anyone who only reads the economic newspapers.

How to Access Unlisted Equity

Access has improved significantly in the past five years. The formal routes include pre-IPO funds run by SEBI-registered managers, direct secondary transactions in unlisted shares through brokers who specialise in this market, and employee stock option transfers where permitted by company articles. For larger allocations, direct negotiation with promoters for a minority stake — structured properly with shareholder agreements and information rights — is possible for investors who have the relationships and the legal infrastructure to support it.

What to check when there is no quarterly report or analyst coverage? The answer is: go back to first principles of business analysis, which is what listed-market investing should be but often isn't.

  • Audited financials for at least five years — not management accounts, audited accounts with a reputable firm. Qualification notes in the auditor's report deserve disproportionate attention.
  • The promoter's track record across business cycles. Have they managed capital responsibly through a downturn? What happened to minority shareholders in previous ventures?
  • Customer concentration and switching costs. A business where the top three customers represent 70% of revenue is a different risk profile from one where the top fifty represent 60%.
  • Working capital discipline. Unlisted companies sometimes mask operational problems through receivables management. Deteriorating debtor days is a red flag that quarterly coverage would surface; in unlisted companies, you need to look for it yourself.
  • The exit pathway. Illiquidity is the price you pay for the pricing anomaly. You should have a clear view of how and when you will exit before you enter.

The Illiquidity Premium: Real but Often Underestimated

The illiquidity premium in unlisted equity is genuine. Academic finance argues it should be 2-4% annualised. My experience in Indian private markets suggests the realised premium, when a position works out, is often larger than this — but the distribution is skewed. The positions that work out well work out very well. The ones that don't work out can be genuinely illiquid for years with no mechanism for price discovery or exit.

This is not an argument against the asset class. It is an argument for entering it only when you have genuine conviction based on primary research, a clearly understood exit thesis, and capital you can afford to have locked up for five to seven years without it affecting your financial life. Unlisted equity is not a substitute for listed equity in a portfolio — it is a complement, for the portion of capital where you have both the knowledge and the patience to accept a fundamentally different return profile.
